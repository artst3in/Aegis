# Why one second?

## The short version

The SOS button waits one second while you hold it. Not three. Not
half a second. One. That number was not picked because it felt
right. It was calculated, the same way everything else in Aegis is.

## Two ways to get it wrong

A panic button can fail in two opposite directions.

Make the hold **too short**, and your phone fires SOS by accident —
a press in your pocket, a squeeze in your bag, a sleeve dragging
across the screen. That is not just embarrassing. Every false alarm
teaches the people watching over you to take the next one less
seriously. Cry wolf three times and a real call for help arrives to
someone who has learned to ignore it. A false alarm does not cost
one apology. It quietly drains the trust the whole system runs on.

Make the hold **too long**, and you fail the other way. In a real
emergency every second you stand there holding still is a second
the alert is late. Worse — if someone is right there trying to stop
you, a long hold hands them time to see what you are doing and knock
the phone out of your hand. Under fear, three seconds feels like
ten. People let go. The alert never sends.

So one number has to sit between two cliffs.

## Where the cliffs are

The chance of an *accidental* hold drops off fast. A bump lasts a
fraction of a second. A pocket press almost never lands on this exact
six-sided button and stays there without sliding off. By half a
second most accidents are already gone. By one second they are
essentially gone.

The cost of *waiting*, on the other hand, grows steadily — one
second late is one second late, two is twice that. There is no
cliff. It just climbs.

| Hold time | Accidental fires | Speed in a real emergency |
|---|---|---|
| 0.5 s | starting to creep in | fastest |
| 1.0 s | essentially none | fast |
| 1.5 s | none | slower than it needs to be |
| 3.0 s | none | feels like forever under fear |

The accident risk falls away by one second. Past that, longer holds
buy almost nothing and cost you speed. The sweet spot is the moment
the accident curve flattens out — and that moment is one second.

## Why you don't have to race

There is a second way to fire SOS that needs no screen at all —
pressing the power button five times. If someone already has your
hands or your phone, that is the path that protects you. Because that
case is covered, the on-screen hold does not have to be a hair-
trigger fighting to beat an attacker's grab. It can sit at the calm,
correct one second instead.

## The one thing that is slower on purpose

Turning an alarm **off** takes three seconds, not one. That is
deliberate. Firing a false alarm is recoverable — you send a quick
"sorry, mistake." But silencing a *real* alarm that you needed is
the disaster. So the off switch is made harder to hit by accident,
on purpose. Fast to call for help. Slow to take it back.

## Why it can't be changed

You cannot adjust this number in settings, and that is not an
oversight. A setting is one more thing to get wrong under stress, one
more thing an abuser could quietly change on your phone, one more way
the most important button in the app could be turned against you. The
math gives one right answer for everyone. We set it, we locked it, and
no one — not you in a calm moment, not someone else in a cruel one —
can move it.

One second. Long enough that your pocket can't betray you. Short
enough to beat the hand reaching for your phone. That is the whole
reason for the number.
